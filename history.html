HISTORY OF MANGA

How did this phenomena start?

Early Roots (12th–18th Century)

The origins of manga can be traced back to Chōjū-jinbutsu-giga (鳥獣人物戯画), a series of picture scrolls from the 12th century that depicted animals with human characteristics in humorous scenes. These scrolls already utilized sequential storytelling, similar to modern manga. During the Edo period (1603–1868), the ukiyo-e (浮世絵, "pictures of the floating world") art style became popular. The painter Hokusai, famous for his woodblock prints, used the term “manga” for a series of his casual sketches in Hokusai Manga (1814), although the meaning was different from today. Another popular format was kibyōshi (illustrated books with satirical content), which closely resembled modern comics.

Meiji Era and Western Influence (1868–1912)

With Japan's modernization, Western influences began to emerge. Rakuten Kitazawa (1876–1955) was among the first artists to use the word "manga" to describe comic stories. Printing technology improved, enabling the creation of illustrated magazines with elements of sequential storytelling.

Pre-war and Wartime Manga (1912–1945)

In the early 20th century, manga began to take its modern form. Artists like Ippei Okamoto and Osamu Tezuka were inspired by American and European comics. During World War II, manga was often used as propaganda, but underground artists continued to create humorous and satirical stories.

Post-war Boom and Osamu Tezuka (1945–1960s)

After the war, manga’s popularity exploded, largely thanks to Osamu Tezuka (1928–1989), who is called the "God of Manga." His work New Treasure Island (新宝島, 1947) introduced cinematic storytelling techniques, dynamic panels, and deeper characters. Among his famous works are Astro Boy (鉄腕アトム, 1952), Black Jack, and Phoenix. His style influenced an entire generation of artists. The phenomenon of rental manga libraries (kashihon manga) also emerged, where future famous authors like Shigeru Mizuki (GeGeGe no Kitaro) started their careers.

Golden Era and the Rise of Shōnen/Shōjo Manga (1960s–1980s)

Manga became mainstream, with magazines like Shōnen Jump (1968) and Shōjo Friend dominating the market. Genres became more defined: Shōnen (少年, for boys) – action and adventure stories (Dragon Ball, Fist of the North Star). Shōjo (少女, for girls) – romance and emotional stories (The Rose of Versailles, Sailor Moon). New genres also appeared: Seinen (青年, for adult men) and Josei (女性, for adult women) – more complex stories (Akira, Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind).

International Popularity and the Anime Boom (1990s–2000s)

In the 1990s, manga became a global phenomenon thanks to anime adaptations (Dragon Ball Z, Sailor Moon, One Piece, Naruto). The "Big Three" (Naruto, Bleach, One Piece) dominated the market. Publishers like Viz Media, Tokyopop, and Kodansha USA helped spread manga in the West.

Digital Era and Modern Trends (2010s–Present)

Digital platforms like Shonen Jump+, Manga Plus, and web manga portals have made content more accessible. The isekai (reincarnation into a fantasy world) genre became extremely popular (Re:Zero, That Time I Got Reincarnated as a Slime). Indie and web manga (One Punch Man started as a webcomic) gained recognition. Manga sales surpassed American comics in many countries, confirming its global dominance.
